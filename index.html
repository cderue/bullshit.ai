Opinion

BullshitAI

Artificial intelligence has become the most commonly deployed, least honestly used technology in corporate history. Here is a field guide to who is actually doing what — and why the gap between those categories is widening faster than most organizations realize.

Every serious technology produces, at the moment of its mainstream adoption, a class of organizations that genuinely deploy it — and a much larger class that performs deployment. They announce it, invest in the optics of it, restructure divisions around it. But they never quite answer the question that separates theater from transformation: what are you actually trying to do, and what would be impossible without this?

AI is no different — except that it is. Because the gap between performance and substance is, with artificial intelligence, not merely a gap of missed efficiency. It is a gap of missed imagination. AI is not a faster database or a cheaper conveyor belt. It is a technology that can genuinely extend the cognitive reach of the people and organizations that use it. Which means the cost of using it badly is not just waste. It is the progressive surrender of a frontier that, once abandoned, is very hard to reclaim.

What follows is not a technical analysis. It is a taxonomy of intent — three archetypes that have crystallized in the real world, each with its own internal logic, its own self-deception, and its own long-term cost. Two of them are, in different ways, forms of organized dishonesty. The third is the only thing worth building.

The central question

"What can your organization do today that it genuinely could not do twelve months ago — not faster, not cheaper, but fundamentally could not do?"

01

Archetype one

The Performers

The Pattern

AI as a content weapon

The first archetype comprises organizations — and increasingly, individuals with organizational reach — for whom AI is primarily an output machine for attention. They generate posts, articles, comments, reports, and white papers at volumes no human team could produce. They optimize for engagement metrics, for the appearance of expertise, for the kind of high-frequency visibility that social platforms reward regardless of the underlying quality of thought.

The more sophisticated among them do not use AI to simply fabricate. They use it to amplify: to take a modest insight and refract it into a hundred variations, each tailored to a slightly different audience segment. The content is not entirely false. It is something more insidious — it is systematically untethered from the actual cost of producing ideas. Real thinking is expensive. It requires friction, uncertainty, revision, and the willingness to be wrong before being right. AI-generated content that simulates the output of thinking without performing any of its work is recognizable — not always consciously, but cumulatively — as hollow.

"The content is not always false. It is something more insidious — it is untethered from the cost of thought."

The Self-Deception

Reach is not resonance

These organizations have understood one real thing: AI dramatically compresses the cost of content production. What they have failed to understand — or refuse to acknowledge — is that the value of content has never resided in its volume or its production cost. It resides in the quality of the attention it captures and the durability of the trust it builds.

The self-deception is metric-driven. Impressions go up. Follower counts grow. Engagement rates hold steady. The dashboard flatters. What the dashboard does not show is the gradual erosion of credibility — the slowly widening gap between the appearance of authority and the reality of it. Audiences sense this before they can articulate it. At some threshold, the trust account runs dry, and no volume of content can refill it. The organizations that reach that threshold rarely understand what happened. They were watching the wrong numbers.

The Strategic Cost

Epistemic debt compounds silently

There is also a cost that extends beyond any individual organization. Every AI-generated article that simulates analysis without performing it, every manufactured opinion that floods a professional platform, every fabricated credential that circulates uncontested — each one marginally degrades the information environment that all organizations depend on to make decisions. This is a tragedy-of-the-commons problem. No single actor feels responsible for it. Everyone suffers from it.

For the organizations themselves, the long-term strategic cost is the devaluation of their own signal. In a world drowning in generated content, the scarcest and most valuable asset becomes authentic expertise demonstrably applied. The Performers are, at scale, destroying the very currency they are trying to accumulate. They are optimizing for short-term reach at the cost of long-term authority — and the exchange rate between the two worsens with every piece of content they publish.

✕

Verdict — Archetype 01

Value is not created here. Attention is briefly captured and trust is slowly spent. The organizations that operate this way are drawing down on a bank account — reputation — that they have no coherent plan to refill. The damage compounds quietly until it does not.

02

Archetype two

The Downsizers

The Pattern

Efficiency as a ceiling, not a floor

The second archetype is, on the surface, more respectable. These are organizations with genuine AI investment, genuine technical implementation, and genuine productivity gains. They are not lying about what they are doing. They use AI to automate tasks, compress timelines, and reduce headcount while maintaining output volumes. By almost any financial metric, the short-term results are positive.

The problem is not what they are doing. The problem is what they have decided not to ask. These organizations — overwhelmingly driven by quarterly shareholder expectations — have adopted a fundamentally reductive mental model of AI. They have asked: what do we currently do, and how can AI help us do it with fewer people? They have not asked: what could we do that we currently cannot, if our people were ten times more capable? That is a different question. A more demanding one. It requires a level of organizational imagination that short-term financial incentives structurally discourage.

"They are using a technology capable of redefining their industry — to trim the edges of their existing one."

The Self-Deception

Transformation language, optimization intent

What makes this archetype particularly opaque is the language it uses about itself. The investor deck says "AI-powered transformation." The press release announces "a new era of intelligent operations." The town hall speaks of "augmenting our workforce with cutting-edge capabilities." But the actual decisions — made quietly, in finance committees and workforce planning sessions — are simpler: same output, fewer people, better margins.

This gap between declared intent and operational reality is not innocent. Employees who survive a reduction round and are told they are now "augmented" by AI understand, at some level, that augmentation was not the primary motivation. That understanding corrodes the trust necessary for genuine AI adoption. People do not bring their best thinking to tools they believe exist to eventually replace them. The very workforce whose domain expertise is needed to make AI investments productive is the one being alienated by the way those investments are communicated and deployed.

The deeper self-deception is strategic. These organizations believe they have an AI strategy because they have AI deployment. They do not. They have an efficiency program with a new set of tools. A strategy implies a vision of a different future — one in which the organization does things it could not previously do. Pure cost optimization, however technically sophisticated, is not a vision. It is the same shareholder-driven logic these companies had before AI arrived, repackaged in the language of the moment.

The Strategic Cost

The compounding cost of unlived potential

The strategic cost here is more subtle than for the Performers, but potentially larger. These organizations are making efficiency gains that their competitors are also making. The delta — the competitive advantage from AI — shrinks toward zero as the technology becomes table stakes. The organizations that will separate themselves are the ones that used AI not just to run the same race faster, but to enter a different race entirely.

There is also a talent cost that does not appear on any balance sheet. The people most capable of helping an organization realize AI's transformative potential — those who understand both the domain deeply and the technology well — are not drawn to organizations that signal, through their AI decisions, a fundamental lack of ambition. They go where the question being asked is bigger. And they compound there, building the capabilities that Downsizer organizations will later try and fail to acquire.

△

Verdict — Archetype 02

Not dishonest in the way the Performers are. But guilty of a category error: confusing the optimization of current capabilities for the development of new ones. These organizations are using a technology capable of expanding what they can conceive of doing — and using it exclusively to do what they already do, slightly more cheaply. The opportunity cost is enormous, and it does not appear on any balance sheet.

Why organizations default to the wrong archetypes

It is worth pausing to ask why. Why do so many organizations, given access to genuinely transformative technology, default to performance or pure optimization? The answer is systemic, not individual — and understanding it is a precondition for escaping it. Three structural forces push organizations toward the wrong archetypes with a consistency that makes them look almost like gravity.

Force 01

Measurement systems reward the visible

Quarterly earnings reward cost reduction and output volume. They do not reward expanded organizational capability, which may cost money in the short term and only pay off over years. The organizations that default to optimization do so in part because their measurement systems make optimization the only legible strategy. What cannot be measured in the next reporting period cannot be justified in the budget cycle.

Force 02

Transformation is harder to explain than efficiency

Telling a board that AI will help your teams do things they have never done before requires a coherent vision of what those things are. That vision requires strategic imagination and the willingness to be held accountable for a future that does not yet exist. Telling a board that AI will reduce your cost-per-output by 30% requires only a spreadsheet. The path of least resistance is always the spreadsheet.

Force 03

Fear drives conservative use

Organizations that feel threatened by AI — whose leaders sense that the technology could disrupt their existing model — tend to deploy it defensively. They use it to shore up what exists rather than to explore what could. This is rational at the individual leadership level and collectively self-defeating. Defensive AI deployment is a way of managing the anxiety of disruption without actually addressing it.

03

Archetype three

The Amplifiers

The Pattern

Expanding the ceiling, not just raising throughput

The third archetype starts from a different premise. Not how do we do what we do more cheaply — but what are our people currently incapable of doing, because of time, cognitive bandwidth, data scale, or speed, that they could do if those constraints were removed?

This is not a philosophical question. It is an operational one with very concrete answers. A team of three engineers who can now reason about a codebase ten times larger than before can take on architectural decisions that were previously out of reach. A coach who can track, in real time, the biomechanical signatures of thirty athletes rather than three can develop interventions at a granularity that changes outcomes. A strategist who can synthesize competitive intelligence across hundreds of sources in the time it previously took to read ten can ask fundamentally different questions about market positioning. In each case, the efficiency gain is real — but it is not the point. The point is that the freed capacity flows toward genuinely harder problems, more ambitious goals, work that was not on the table before.

"The freed capacity flows into harder problems, more ambitious goals — work that simply was not on the table before."

What Makes It Work

Trust, literacy, and the redefinition of success

Amplifier organizations are not distinguished primarily by their technical infrastructure. They are distinguished by the clarity of the contract they have made with their people about what AI is for. When employees understand that AI investment exists to expand their individual capabilities — not to create a redundancy argument against them — adoption becomes genuine rather than performative. People bring their domain expertise into the AI interaction rather than treating it as an external tool to be monitored with suspicion. That expertise is irreplaceable; it is what makes the AI interaction valuable rather than generic.

This requires investment in AI literacy that is broader than most organizations attempt. Not training every employee to build models. But ensuring that every employee understands, in the context of their specific role, where AI adds genuine leverage and where human judgment remains irreplaceable. That understanding does not emerge from a half-day workshop. It emerges from deliberate, ongoing, domain-specific practice — which requires that the organization create the space and the culture for that practice to happen.

These organizations also redefine their success metrics. They do not only ask "how much faster?" They ask "what are we attempting now that we were not attempting last year?" The shift from throughput metrics to frontier metrics is subtle but decisive. It changes what leaders celebrate, what teams are rewarded for, and consequently what they try. And over time, what people try is the only thing that actually determines what an organization becomes.

The Long Game

Compounding ambition

The compounding logic here is the inverse of the Performers' compounding problem. As Amplifier organizations build frontier capability, they attract the talent most interested in hard problems. As they accumulate results from that work, they develop institutional knowledge about how to push further — what kinds of AI-human collaboration yield genuine breakthroughs, where the models fall short and human judgment is irreplaceable, how to structure the iterative loop between AI assistance and expert validation. This knowledge is not easily acquired from outside. It is the product of actually doing the work, at the frontier, for long enough to understand it.

This is not utopian. Amplifier organizations still make bad AI decisions. They still have employees who resist change, leaders who default to conservative deployment, and projects that fail to deliver on their ambition. The difference is the direction of travel — and the fact that the direction of travel, compounded over time, is the thing that determines everything.

✓

Verdict — Archetype 03

The only archetype that treats AI as what it actually is: a lever for expanding the frontier of what an organization can do, not just how efficiently it does what it already does. Not without its own failures and frictions — but oriented in the right direction, which over time is everything.

Self-Assessment

Where does your organization actually sit?

Most organizations believe they are Amplifiers. Most are not. The following questions are diagnostic, not rhetorical. Honest answers locate you more reliably than any strategy document or investor deck ever will.

  • Q1 What specific capability does your organization have today, enabled by AI, that it did not have eighteen months ago — not faster execution of the same tasks, but a genuinely new capability that previously did not exist?
  • Q2 When you communicate AI investment internally, do employees understand it as something that expands what they can do — or as something that makes their role more precarious? Ask them directly. Their answer will be more accurate than yours.
  • Q3 What is the hardest problem your best people are working on because AI has freed capacity from work they used to spend time on? If the answer is "they're doing more of the same work," you have your answer.
  • Q4 In your last significant AI investment decision, was the primary driver cost reduction, output increase — or the expansion of what your teams could attempt? Which of those three appears first in the business case?
  • Q5 Do your AI metrics measure efficiency and volume — or the ambition and novelty of the work your people are now doing? What you measure is what you are actually optimizing for, regardless of what your strategy documents say.

Conclusion

The window
is open.
Not forever.

We are at a moment in AI's development where the choices organizations make about how to use it are still largely reversible. A company that has deployed AI primarily as a content engine can, with sufficient leadership will, reorient toward genuine amplification. A company that has used AI to justify headcount reduction can build, alongside that, a program aimed at genuine capability expansion. The path is hard, but it exists.

What closes that window is not time or technology — it is culture. Cultures calcify around the first choices they make about a technology. The organization that signals, through its earliest AI decisions, that the primary purpose of artificial intelligence is to reduce the cost of human labor will find it extraordinarily difficult to build a culture of AI-enabled ambition three years from now. The signal has already been sent. The contract has already been written. People have already drawn their conclusions about what AI means for them in this organization — and those conclusions do not easily reverse.

This is why the distinction between the three archetypes is not primarily ethical, though it has ethical dimensions. It is strategic. The Performers are destroying their credibility in slow motion. The Downsizers are optimizing their way to competitive parity while ceding the frontier to organizations willing to ask harder questions. And the Amplifiers are doing something that will be very difficult to replicate from behind: they are building the organizational muscle, the cultural trust, and the institutional knowledge to do things that have not been done before.

The technology is indifferent to which category you choose. It will serve all three archetypes with equal capability and equal indifference. The choice is entirely yours — and it is, right now, still yours to make.

Stop asking how AI can help you do what you already do.
Start asking what you would dare to attempt
if your people's capabilities had no ceiling.
